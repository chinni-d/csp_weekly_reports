### Weekly Report
- **Objective:** To improve the physical environment of the aquaculture farm.
- **Detailed Report:** During the second week, our focus shifted toward the physical maintenance and hygiene of the aquaculture farms. A clean farm environment is the first line of defense against diseases. We spent several days assisting farmers in clearing excessive weeds and wild vegetation from the pond embankments. These weeds often harbor pests and predators like snakes and crabs that can damage the pond structure or prey on the fish fry. We also inspected the "bunds" or embankments for any signs of soil erosion or leaks, as even a small leak can lead to significant water and stock loss. Beyond the ponds, we worked on cleaning the general farm surroundings, ensuring that plastic waste and other debris were properly disposed of. This activity not only improved the farm's efficiency but also significantly enhanced the neighborhood's aesthetics. Keeping the workspace tidy helps farmers move around more safely, especially during the early morning hours when visibility is low. We discussed the "Sense of Belonging" mentioned in our project goals, explaining to the farmers how a well-maintained farm reflects pride in their community. The physical labor involved gave us a deep appreciation for the hard work aquaculture farmers put in daily. ### Weekly Report
- **Objective:** To monitor and maintain optimal water conditions.
- **Detailed Report:** This week was dedicated to the most critical aspect of aquaculture: Water Quality Management. Fish depend entirely on their aquatic environment for oxygen and health. We assisted farmers in performing daily checks of water parameters like pH levels, temperature, and turbidity. Using simple testing kits, we measured Dissolved Oxygen (DO) levels during the early morning hours, which is the most critical time for fish survival. We noticed that several ponds had high ammonia levels due to over-accumulation of organic waste at the bottom. To address this, we helped in the partial water exchange process, carefully monitoring the inflow and outflow to avoid stressing the fish. We also learned about the importance of "liming" the ponds. Applying agricultural lime helps neutralize soil acidity and maintains a stable pH, which is essential for the growth of natural plankton (fish food). As students of Data Science, we started a logbook for the farmers to record these daily variations. This habit of data logging will eventually help them predict potential water quality crashes before they happen. This technical exposure was eye-opening, as it combined biological science with logical monitoring. It taught us how small variations in environmental data can have huge impacts on biological systems.

### Weekly Report
- **Objective:** To assist in efficient feeding and nutrition management.
- **Detailed Report:** Week four was focused on feeding strategies and nutritional management. Feed accounts for nearly 60-70% of the total production cost in aquaculture, making it a vital area for optimization. We assisted the farmers in weighing and measuring the daily ration based on the estimated biomass of the fish in the pond. We observed two types of feeding: floating pellets and sinking pellets. We learned that overfeeding is not only a financial loss but also a major pollutant that degrades water quality. We helped implement a more precise "broadcasting" technique, ensuring feed reaches all parts of the pond so that smaller fish also get their share. This prevents growth variation across the stock. A key lesson this week was the Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR). We explained to the farmers how tracking the amount of feed given versus the weight gained by the fish can help them calculate their profit margins more accurately. Applying our computer science mindset, we showed them how even a small 0.1 improvement in FCR could save thousands of rupees over a harvest cycle. We also cleaned and organized the feed storage rooms to prevent contamination from moisture or rodents. This week's activities highlighted the intersection of biology, economics, and hard physical labor.

### Weekly Report
- **Objective:** To implement and learn about bio-security measures.
- **Detailed Report:** As we entered the fifth week, our focus was on "Bio-security" and disease prevention. In aquaculture, once a disease spreads, it is very difficult to stop, so prevention is the only sustainable strategy. We assisted farmers in inspecting their stock for any unusual behavior or physical marks like red spots or tail rot. We learned about the importance of disinfecting tools like nets and buckets before moving them between different ponds to avoid cross-contamination. We also spent time checking the bird fencing and overhead nets. Birds are not only predators but also carriers of parasites and viruses from one farm to another. One of the most interesting activities was the application of soil and water probiotics. These are beneficial bacteria that out-compete harmful pathogens and help decompose organic waste naturally. This "green" approach reduces the dependency on harmful antibiotics and chemicals, making the harvest safer for human consumption. We held a small awareness session for a group of five farmers, discussing these sustainable methods. It was a great exercise in public speaking and community leadership. We learned that building trust with the community is essential before they accept new technical recommendations.
